%% =================================================================
%%  3. WHAT IS AN MFG
%% =================================================================
\begin{frame}{What is a Mean Field Game?}
A large population of \alert{rational agents}, each minimising a cost
that depends on the population distribution.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{columns}[c]
\begin{column}{0.55\textwidth}
At Nash equilibrium, $(\varphi, \rho)$ solves
\begin{align*}
  -\partial_t\varphi - \nu\Delta\varphi + H(x,\rho,\nabla\varphi) &= 0 \\
  \partial_t\rho - \nu\Delta\rho - \Div(\rho\,\nabla_p H) &= 0
\end{align*}

\medskip
\small
\teal{$\varphi$:} value function (HJB, backward) \\
\teal{$\rho$:} population density (FP, forward) \\
\teal{$H$:} Hamiltonian, encodes cost \& interactions \\
\teal{$\nu$:} diffusion (stochastic noise)
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.43\textwidth}
\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=0.85, font=\small]
  \node[draw=kaustblue, fill=kaustblue!10, rounded corners, minimum width=3.6cm,
        minimum height=1cm, align=center] (HJB)
        at (0,1.6) {\textbf{HJB equation}\\(individual optimisation)};
  \node[draw=kaustteal, fill=kaustteal!10, rounded corners, minimum width=3.6cm,
        minimum height=1cm, align=center] (FP)
        at (0,-0.4) {\textbf{Fokker-Planck}\\(population evolution)};
  \draw[-{Latex[length=2.5mm]}, kaustblue, thick]
        (HJB.east) to[bend left=30] node[right, font=\scriptsize, kaustblue]
        {$\nabla\varphi$ defines drift} (FP.east);
  \draw[-{Latex[length=2.5mm]}, kaustteal, thick]
        (FP.west) to[bend left=30] node[left, font=\scriptsize, kaustteal]
        {$\rho$ enters cost} (HJB.west);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{column}
\end{columns}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\small
\textbf{Applications:} autonomous traffic, financial markets, energy grids,
multi-agent reinforcement learning.
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (2 min):
Mean Field Games model a continuum of rational agents. Each agent minimises
a cost that depends on what the rest of the population is doing.
At Nash equilibrium, no single agent can improve by changing strategy alone.
Mathematically, the equilibrium is described by two coupled PDEs.
The first is a Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation, backward in time, governing
each agent's value function phi. The second is a Fokker-Planck equation, forward
in time, that propagates the population density rho.
The two are coupled: the gradient of phi tells agents where to go, which sets the
drift in the FP equation; and the density rho enters the cost in the HJB.
The coupling object H is the Hamiltonian.
This system shows up in autonomous traffic, financial markets, energy grids,
and increasingly in multi-agent reinforcement learning. The computational challenge
is solving the system in high dimensions, which is where deep learning enters.
}

%% =================================================================
%%  4. TWO FAMILIES
%% =================================================================
\begin{frame}{Two families of deep MFG solvers}
\small
\begin{columns}[T]
\begin{column}{0.49\textwidth}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=kaustblue!8, colframe=kaustblue,
                  title={\textbf{GAN-based} \scriptsize(APAC-Net, MFGAN)},
                  fonttitle=\bfseries]
\textbf{Strengths}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
  \item $\rho$ learned \emph{distributionally} via generator
  \item Wasserstein convergence
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Weakness:} require \alert{separable} $H = H_0(x,p) - f_0(x,\rho)$.
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.49\textwidth}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=kaustteal!8, colframe=kaustteal,
                  title={\textbf{Physics-informed} \scriptsize(MFDGM)},
                  fonttitle=\bfseries]
\textbf{Strengths}
\begin{itemize}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
  \item Handles \emph{any} Hamiltonian
  \item Mature deep PDE machinery
\end{itemize}
\textbf{Weakness:} only \alert{$L^p$ pointwise} ($p<2$); \alert{no convergence rate}.
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{column}
\end{columns}

\medskip
\begin{center}
\textbf{The dichotomy:} \;
power \alert{or} generality, never both.\\
\teal{We resolve it in a precise \emph{weakly non-separable} regime.}
\end{center}
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (2 min):
Existing deep solvers fall into two camps.
On the left, GAN-based methods like APAC-Net and MFGAN. They learn the density rho
distributionally through a generator network, like a Wasserstein GAN. They come with
strong convergence guarantees in the Wasserstein metric. But they only work when the
Hamiltonian is separable, meaning H splits as H\_0 of (x, p) minus f\_0 of (x, rho)
with no joint dependence. This is a hard structural requirement, not a convenience.
The Hopf formula that underlies their saddle-point reformulation breaks if there is
joint dependence.
On the right, physics-informed methods like MFDGM. They directly minimise PDE
residuals using neural networks. They handle any Hamiltonian, separable or not.
But they pay a price: only L\^{}p pointwise convergence for p less than two, and no
explicit convergence rate at all.
The dichotomy is real: power or generality, never both. Our paper shows that within
a precise regime that we call weakly non-separable, you can have both.
}

%%  5. HARD EXAMPLE
%% =================================================================
\begin{frame}{The canonical hard example: MFG-LWR}
\begin{center}
\Large
\colorbox{kaustblue!8}{$\;H(x,\rho,p) = \dfrac{1}{2}\norm{p}^2 - (1-\rho)\,p\;$}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Models traffic flow on a road \cite{huang2019mfg}.
  \item The term \alert{$(1-\rho)\,p$} couples density and momentum jointly.
  \item APAC-Net \alert{cannot} be applied: the Hopf formula breaks.
  \item MFDGM works, but with no rate.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.4cm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=accent!10, colframe=accent, arc=3pt]
\centering
\textbf{Question of this talk:} can we build a GAN-based solver
that handles this Hamiltonian, with an \emph{explicit, sharp} convergence rate?
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (1.5 min):
Here is the canonical hard example. The MFG-LWR traffic flow Hamiltonian
combines a kinetic-energy term, half p squared, with a coupling term,
minus one minus rho times p. That second term has rho and p multiplied
together. They are coupled. There is no way to write H as H\_0 of p
minus f\_0 of rho.
This Hamiltonian models a real situation: traffic on a highway. Density and
momentum interact because cars slow down when the road is congested. So this
matters for autonomous vehicles, congestion pricing, all of that.
Because of the joint dependence, APAC-Net cannot be applied directly. The Hopf
formula they rely on breaks. MFDGM handles it but you get no convergence rate.
The question of this talk is: can we build a GAN-based solver that handles
exactly this Hamiltonian, with an explicit, provably sharp convergence rate?
The answer is yes, in a precise regime that we will define.
}

\begin{frame}{The key insight}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=accent!8, colframe=accent, arc=4pt,
                  fonttitle=\bfseries, title=One observation that changes everything]
\large\centering
If $\rho$ is held \emph{fixed}, any non-separable $H$ becomes \emph{separable}.
\end{tcolorbox}

\vspace{0.4cm}
\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\small, node distance=0.5cm]
  \node[draw=kaustblue, fill=kaustblue!10, rounded corners, minimum width=3cm,
        minimum height=1.2cm, align=center] (full)
        {\textbf{Full non-sep.\ MFG}\\$H(x,\rho,p)$};
  \node[right=2cm of full, draw=kaustteal, fill=kaustteal!10, rounded corners,
        minimum width=3cm, minimum height=1.2cm, align=center] (sub)
        {\textbf{Separable subproblem}\\$H^k(x,p) = H_0 + \eps R(x,\rho^k,p)$};
  \node[right=2cm of sub, draw=kaustgold, fill=kaustgold!10, rounded corners,
        minimum width=3cm, minimum height=1.2cm, align=center] (out)
        {\textbf{Solve via APAC-Net}\\get $\rho^{k+1}$};
  \draw[-{Latex[length=3mm]}, ultra thick, kaustblue]
       (full) -- node[above, font=\footnotesize\itshape] {freeze $\rho$ at $\rho^k$} (sub);
  \draw[-{Latex[length=3mm]}, ultra thick, kaustteal]
       (sub) -- node[above, font=\footnotesize\itshape] {separable!} (out);
  \draw[-{Latex[length=3mm]}, ultra thick, kaustgold, dashed]
       (out.south) to[bend left=40] node[below, font=\footnotesize\itshape]
       {update $\rho^k \leftarrow \rho^{k+1}$, repeat} (full.south);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Each iteration solves a \emph{separable} MFG; APAC-Net applies directly.
  \item Question: \alert{when does the outer iteration converge, and how fast?}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (2 min):
Here is the key insight, and it is elementary.
If you hold rho fixed, any non-separable Hamiltonian becomes separable.
Look at the diagram. We start with the full non-separable MFG on the left.
We freeze rho at the current iterate rho-k. Then the effective Hamiltonian
H-k of x and p is just H-zero plus epsilon times R evaluated at rho-k.
Crucially, this is now a function only of x and p. It is separable in the
sense the GAN solvers require.
So we can apply APAC-Net to the separable subproblem, get a new rho-k-plus-one,
update, and repeat.
This is essentially a Picard fixed-point iteration on the population density,
where each step is one APAC-Net solve.
The whole question of this paper is then: \emph{when does this outer iteration
converge}, and \emph{how fast}? That is what the rest of the talk addresses.
}

%% =================================================================
%%  7. WEAKLY NON-SEPARABLE
%% =================================================================
\begin{frame}{Weakly non-separable Hamiltonians}
\begin{block}{Definition}
$H$ is \emph{weakly non-separable with strength $\eps \geq 0$} if
\[
  H(x,\rho,p) = \underbrace{H_0(x,p)}_{\text{convex, }c_0\text{-strong}}
              - \underbrace{f_0(x,\rho)}_{\substack{\text{Lasry-Lions}\\\text{monotone, const.\ }\lambda}}
              + \;\eps\;\underbrace{R(x,\rho,p)}_{\substack{\text{Lipschitz}\\\text{const.\ }L_R, L_{R,p}}}
\]
\end{block}

\vspace{0.2cm}

\begin{itemize}
  \item $\eps = 0$ recovers the classical \emph{separable} MFG.
  \item Bilinear coupling $R = \rho\cdot p$ recovers the \emph{traffic flow} model.
  \item Density-only coupling $R = R(x,\rho)$ has $L_{R,p} = 0$.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.2cm}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=kaustteal!8, colframe=kaustteal,
                  title=\textbf{GAN-tractable regime}, fonttitle=\bfseries]
\centering\Large
$\eps\, L_R \;<\; \lambda$
\end{tcolorbox}
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (2 min):
Let me be precise about the class.
A Hamiltonian is weakly non-separable with coupling strength epsilon if it
decomposes into three pieces. First, a strongly convex base H-zero of x and p
with convexity constant c-zero. Second, a Lasry-Lions monotone interaction
f-zero of x and rho with monotonicity constant lambda. Third, a coupling
residual R that is Lipschitz in rho with constant L-R and Lipschitz in p with
constant L-R-p, scaled by epsilon.
When epsilon equals zero, you recover the standard separable MFG. When R equals
rho times p you recover the traffic flow model from earlier. When R depends only
on rho, the constant L-R-p is zero.
The single most important quantity is the box at the bottom: epsilon times L-R
strictly less than lambda. We call this the \emph{GAN-tractable regime}.
Intuitively: the coupling strength must be controlled by the restoring force
of the monotone interaction. Below this threshold, our method works. Above it,
it does not. We will see both halves of this in the next slides.
}

%% =================================================================
%%  8. ALGORITHM
%% =================================================================
\begin{frame}{The Extended-MFGAN algorithm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=kaustblue!5, colframe=kaustblue, arc=3pt]
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Hamiltonian $H = H_0 - f_0 + \eps R$, GAN solver, outer iters $K$
\State Initialise $\rho^0 \leftarrow \rho_0$
\For{$k = 0, 1, \ldots, K-1$}
  \State Build effective Hamiltonian:
  $\;H^k(x,p) \leftarrow H_0(x,p) + \eps\, R(x,\rho^k(x,t),p)$
  \State Run inner GAN on the separable MFG with $H^k$:
  \[
    (\rho^{k+1}, \varphi^{k+1}) \leftarrow \textsc{APAC-Net}(H^k,\, f_0,\, g,\, \rho_0)
  \]
\EndFor
\State \Return $(\rho^K, \varphi^K)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{tcolorbox}

\vspace{0.4cm}
\begin{columns}[t]
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\textcolor{kaustblue}{\textbf{Cost}}: $K$ APAC-Net runs.\\
\textcolor{kaustblue}{\textbf{Outer iters needed}}:
$K = \left\lceil \frac{\log(1/\delta)}{\log(1/\kappa)} \right\rceil$ for tolerance $\delta$.
\end{column}
\begin{column}{0.48\textwidth}
\textcolor{kaustteal}{\textbf{Reduces to APAC-Net at $\eps=0$}}:\\
$H^k \equiv H_0$, the outer loop converges in one step.
\end{column}
\end{columns}
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (2 min):
The algorithm is essentially this short pseudocode. Initialise rho-zero to be
the initial density. Then for k from zero to K-minus-one: build the effective
Hamiltonian H-k by plugging in the current rho-k into the coupling residual R.
Then run APAC-Net on the resulting separable MFG. The output is rho-k-plus-one.
Loop.
The cost is K times one APAC-Net run.
How many outer iterations do we need? Once we prove the geometric rate kappa,
to reach tolerance delta we need K equal to the ceiling of log of one over
delta divided by log of one over kappa. So K is small in practice.
A nice sanity check: at epsilon equals zero, the effective Hamiltonian is just
H-zero. There is no rho-bar dependence. The outer loop converges in literally
one iteration. So Extended-MFGAN \emph{reduces exactly to APAC-Net} in the
separable limit. We are interpolating between APAC-Net and the non-separable
regime.
}

%% =================================================================
%%  9. MAIN THEOREM
%% =================================================================
\begin{frame}{The main result}
\begin{resultbox}[title=Theorem (Wasserstein-2 contraction)]
Under the GAN-tractable condition $\eps L_R < \lambda$:
\[
  \norm{\rho^{k+1} - \rho^*}_{L^2}
  \;\;\leq\;\;
  \kappa\;\norm{\rho^k - \rho^*}_{L^2},
  \qquad
  \boxed{\;\kappa = \dfrac{\eps\, L_R}{\lambda}\;}
\]
Consequently $\rho^k \to \rho^*$ in the Wasserstein-2 metric at the geometric
rate $\kappa^k$.
\end{resultbox}

\vspace{0.4cm}
This is the \alert{first} explicit convergence rate for any deep-learning
MFG solver in the non-separable setting.

\vspace{0.3cm}

\begin{itemize}
  \item Both $L^2$ and $\W_2$ convergence (a strict improvement over MFDGM).
  \item Rate is \emph{explicit}: depends only on the data $\eps, L_R, \lambda$.
  \item In the next slide, we sketch the proof.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (2 min):
Here is the main result.
Under the GAN-tractable condition epsilon times L-R strictly less than lambda,
the Extended-MFGAN outer iteration is a contraction in L-squared with rate
exactly kappa equals epsilon times L-R over lambda.
In particular, rho-k converges to the equilibrium rho-star at the geometric
rate kappa-to-the-k.
This is the first explicit convergence rate for any deep-learning MFG solver
in the non-separable setting. APAC-Net does not give a rate; MFDGM does not
give a rate. We do.
The convergence is in L-squared, which by a Brenier-Benamou bound under the
density bounds gives Wasserstein-two convergence at the same rate. So we
strictly improve on MFDGM, which only gets L-p for p less than two.
The rate is also \emph{explicit}, meaning it is a closed-form function of the
problem data. You can read off how many iterations you need.
The next slide sketches how the proof works.
}

%% =================================================================
%%  10. PROOF SKETCH
%% =================================================================
\begin{frame}{Proof sketch: Lasry-Lions duality}
\textbf{Compute} $\;\dfrac{d}{dt}\displaystyle\int_\Omega \delta\varphi\cdot\delta\rho\,dx$
\;where $\delta\varphi = \varphi_1 - \varphi_2$, $\delta\rho = \rho_1 - \rho_2$.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{enumerate}
\setlength\itemsep{0.15em}
  \item Substitute HJB and FP for $\varphi_i, \rho_i$.
  \item Diffusion terms $\nu\Delta$ \alert{cancel} between HJB and FP.
  \item Integrate by parts on the current term.
  \item Integrate over $[0,T]$; boundary terms \alert{vanish}
        ($\delta\rho(0)=0$, $\delta\varphi(T)=0$).
  \item Decompose: \;
        $A_0+C_0$ (convex base) \,$+\,\eps(A_R+C_R)$ (coupling).
  \item Combine with monotonicity of $f_0$.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=accent!10, colframe=accent, arc=3pt]
\centering
$\lambda\,\norm{\delta\rho}^2_{L^2}
 \;\leq\;
 \eps\, L_R\,\norm{\delta\bar\rho}_{L^2}\,\norm{\delta\rho}_{L^2}.$
\end{tcolorbox}

\vspace{0.2cm}
$\Rightarrow$ Banach Fixed-Point Theorem: contraction at rate $\kappa = \eps L_R/\lambda$. \;$\square$
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (3 min):
The proof technique is Lasry-Lions duality. This is the same technique used to
prove uniqueness in the classical separable MFG. We adapt it to the perturbed
non-separable setting.
The whole proof is six steps. Take two MFG solutions, with densities rho-one
and rho-two, value functions phi-one and phi-two. Compute the time derivative
of the inner product of delta-phi with delta-rho.
Step 1: substitute the HJB and FP equations.
Step 2: the diffusion terms nu times Laplacian cancel exactly between the
two equations. This is the magical part.
Step 3: integrate by parts on the current term.
Step 4: integrate from time zero to T. Boundary terms vanish because of the
boundary conditions: delta-rho is zero at time zero, delta-phi is zero at
time T.
Step 5: split the resulting Hamiltonian-difference into the contribution from
H-zero (the convex base, gives a non-positive term by strong convexity) and
the contribution from the residual R (gives a coupling term controlled by L-R).
Step 6: combine with the Lasry-Lions monotonicity of f-zero.
The final estimate is the boxed inequality: lambda times the L-squared norm
squared of delta-rho is at most epsilon times L-R times the product of the
input and output L-squared norms.
Dividing by the L-squared norm of delta-rho gives the contraction.
Then Banach fixed-point theorem gives geometric convergence.
}

%% =================================================================
%%  11. SHARPNESS
%% =================================================================
\begin{frame}{Sharpness: rate is tight}
\begin{block}{Proposition (bilinear exactness)}
For $R(x,\rho,p) = \rho\cdot p$, the linearised outer fixed-point map at the
uniform equilibrium has operator norm \emph{exactly}
\[
  \kappa = \dfrac{\eps L_R}{\lambda}.
\]
\end{block}

\vspace{0.3cm}

\textbf{Why?} \;On the torus at $\rho^* = 1$:
\begin{align*}
  \text{Stationary FP:} \quad & \Delta(\delta\varphi) = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \nabla\delta\varphi = 0\\
  \text{Linearised HJB:} \quad & -\lambda\,\delta\rho + \eps L_R\,\delta\bar\rho = 0\\[4pt]
  \implies \quad & \boxed{\,\delta\rho \;=\; \dfrac{\eps L_R}{\lambda}\,\delta\bar\rho\,}
\end{align*}

\vspace{0.2cm}

The Theorem cannot be improved: the upper bound is \emph{attained}.
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (1.5 min):
Now I want to convince you the rate kappa is the right rate, not just an upper
bound. We prove this via a direct linearisation calculation on the bilinear
case R equals rho times p.
Take the ergodic MFG on the torus. The unique uniform equilibrium has rho-star
identically one. Linearise the outer fixed-point map at this point.
The stationary Fokker-Planck equation reduces to Laplacian of delta-phi equals
zero. On the torus this forces the gradient of delta-phi to vanish.
With that, the linearised HJB collapses to the scalar relation
minus lambda times delta-rho plus epsilon times L-R times delta-rho-bar equals zero.
Solving: delta-rho equals epsilon L-R over lambda times delta-rho-bar.
That is the operator norm of the linearised map. Exactly kappa. So the upper
bound in the theorem is attained. It is not just a bound; it is the rate.
}

%% =================================================================
%%  12. NECESSITY
%% =================================================================
\begin{frame}{Necessity: the condition is required}
\begin{block}{Proposition (necessity counterexample)}
For the same ergodic MFG: when $\eps L_R \geq \lambda$, the linearised outer
iteration \emph{fails} to contract. \\
It \alert{stagnates} at $\eps L_R = \lambda$ and \alert{grows geometrically}
when $\eps L_R > \lambda$.
\end{block}

\vspace{0.4cm}
\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\small]
  \draw[->, thick] (-0.3, 0) -- (8, 0) node[below right] {$\eps L_R / \lambda$};
  \draw[->, thick] (0, -0.3) -- (0, 2.4) node[above left] {$\|\rho^k - \rho^*\|$};
  \draw[kaustteal, very thick, smooth, samples=80, domain=0:0.95]
        plot (\x*4, {1.8 * (1-\x)^4});
  \draw[accent, very thick, smooth, samples=80, domain=1.05:1.4]
        plot (\x*4, {0.4 + (\x - 1.0)*5});
  \draw[dashed, gray, thick] (4, 0) -- (4, 2.4);
  \node[below, kaustteal] at (1.5, -0.05) {\textbf{converging}};
  \node[below, accent] at (5.6, -0.05) {\textbf{diverging}};
  \node[above, gray] at (4, 2.5) {$\eps^* = \lambda/L_R$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\textbf{Combined:} the GAN-tractable condition is \alert{necessary and sufficient}
for linear convergence (linearised regime).
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (1.5 min):
The previous slide showed the rate is tight. This slide goes one step further:
the condition is \emph{necessary}. Without it, the iteration does not converge.
Same ergodic MFG, same linearisation. When epsilon times L-R is greater than
or equal to lambda, the operator norm is at least one. The error does not decay.
At the threshold it stagnates. Above the threshold it grows geometrically and
the iteration diverges from every starting point near the equilibrium.
The cartoon at the bottom captures it: a sharp phase transition at epsilon-star
equal to lambda over L-R. To the left, geometric decay. To the right, geometric
growth.
Combined with the previous slide: the GAN-tractable condition is both necessary
and sufficient for linear convergence in the linearised regime. That is unusual
for a contraction theorem. Most contraction theorems give a bound; ours gives
the actual rate.
}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (2 min):
Second: the bilinear MFG-LWR traffic flow. Now there is real structure to verify.
For R equals rho times p, when you linearise at the uniform equilibrium, the

which is the magnitude of p, equals the absolute value of one minus epsilon.

equals one half, and decays back to zero at epsilon equals one.
For lambda equal to point two, the equation kappa equals one has two roots in

Right panel: convergence curves for each epsilon. Below the first threshold and
above the second: geometric decay. Between them: geometric growth.
Empirical and theoretical rates agree to four digits across all eleven values
of epsilon.

\begin{frame}{Contributions in one slide}
\begin{enumerate}
\setlength\itemsep{0.5em}
  \item \textcolor{kaustblue}{\textbf{Weakly non-separable Hamiltonians.}}\\
        A formal interpolation between separable and non-separable MFGs.
  \item \textcolor{kaustblue}{\textbf{Extended-MFGAN.}}\\
        Decomposes a non-separable MFG into a sequence of separable subproblems,
        each solved by APAC-Net.
  \item \textcolor{kaustteal}{\textbf{Wasserstein-2 contraction theorem.}}\\
        Explicit rate $\kappa = \eps L_R/\lambda$.
        First explicit rate for any deep MFG solver in the non-separable setting.
  \item \textcolor{kaustteal}{\textbf{Sharpness.}}\\
        Rate is tight in the bilinear case; condition is necessary.
  \item \textcolor{kaustgold}{\textbf{Numerics.}}\\
        Empirical $\kappa$ matches theory to \alert{four digits} across three benchmarks.
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\note{
\textbf{Speaker} (1 min):
Five contributions, in one slide.
First: a new class of Hamiltonians, weakly non-separable, that interpolates
between the separable and non-separable cases.
Second: a new algorithm, Extended-MFGAN, that decomposes a non-separable
MFG into separable subproblems solvable by APAC-Net.
Third: a Wasserstein-two contraction theorem with explicit rate kappa equals
epsilon L-R over lambda. The first explicit rate for any deep MFG solver
in this setting.
Fourth: that rate is sharp, and the condition is necessary.
Fifth: numerical experiments where the empirical contraction rate matches
the theoretical rate to four decimal places. Across three different benchmarks.
}
