\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Mean Field Games (MFGs) \citep{lasry2007mean, huang2006large} have emerged as
a fundamental tool for modeling the collective behavior of large populations
of rational agents — from autonomous vehicles navigating congested roads
\citep{huang2019mfg} to investors competing in financial markets
\citep{carmona2018probabilistic} and agents in industrial and engineering systems.
At equilibrium, each agent plays optimally against the aggregate effect of the
population, yielding a Nash equilibrium described by a coupled system of
a backward Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation and a forward Fokker-Planck
(FP) equation:
\begin{align}
  -\partial_t \varphi - \nu\Delta\varphi + H(x,\rho,\nabla\varphi) &= 0,
  \quad \text{in } (0,T)\times\Omega, \label{eq:HJB}\\
  \partial_t \rho - \nu\Delta\rho - \Div\!\bigl(\rho\,\nabla_p H(x,\rho,\nabla\varphi)\bigr)
  &= 0, \quad \text{in } (0,T)\times\Omega, \label{eq:FP}
\end{align}
where $\varphi$ is the agents' value function, $\rho$ is the population density,
and $H$ is the Hamiltonian encoding the game's interaction structure.
Solving this system in high dimensions is a central computational challenge
in the field.

\paragraph{The power and limits of GAN-based solvers.}
A major advance came with the observation that, when the Hamiltonian is
\emph{separable} — i.e., when $H(x,\rho,p) = H_0(x,p) - f_0(x,\rho)$,
decoupling dynamics from congestion — the MFG system can be reformulated
as a min-max saddle-point problem \citep{cao2021connecting}.
This connects MFGs structurally to Wasserstein GANs, enabling
algorithms such as APAC-Net \citep{lin2021apac} and MFGANs
\citep{ruthotto2020machine} that learn the equilibrium density
\emph{distributionally} (via a generator network) and converge in the
Wasserstein metric — a strictly stronger guarantee than pointwise approximation.

However, the separability assumption is not merely a technical convenience:
it is a hard structural requirement.
The Hopf formula, which underlies the GAN reformulation, breaks down the moment
$H$ couples $\rho$ and $p$ jointly.
Many of the most important MFG models in practice are \emph{non-separable}.
The canonical example is the MFG-LWR traffic flow model \citep{huang2019mfg},
where congestion enters the dynamics through the term $(1-\rho)p$,
yielding a Hamiltonian
\[
  H(x,\rho,p) = \tfrac{1}{2}\norm{p}^2 - (1-\rho)\,p
\]
that couples density and momentum inseparably.
GAN-based methods cannot be applied to such problems.

\paragraph{The cost of going beyond separability.}
The recent MFDGM method \citep{assouline2023deep} addresses non-separable
Hamiltonians by directly minimizing PDE residuals via physics-informed
neural networks, removing any structural requirement on $H$.
This is a genuine advance, but it comes at a price:
MFDGM approximates $\rho$ \emph{pointwise} rather than distributionally,
achieves only $L^p$ ($p<2$) convergence with no explicit rate,
and loses the Wasserstein-level guarantees that make GAN methods
statistically and geometrically meaningful.
The state of the art thus faces a stark dichotomy:
GAN methods are powerful but restricted;
physics-informed methods are general but weaker.

\paragraph{This paper: resolving the dichotomy.}
We show that this dichotomy is not fundamental — it is a consequence of
treating the non-separable Hamiltonian as monolithic.
Our key insight is elementary: \emph{if the population density $\rho$ is
temporarily frozen, any non-separable Hamiltonian becomes separable.}
This observation turns the non-separable MFG into a sequence of separable
subproblems, each solvable by existing GAN methods, with the density
updated between iterations.

To make this precise, we introduce the class of
\emph{weakly non-separable Hamiltonians}, where the coupling between $\rho$
and $p$ is parameterized by a strength $\eps \geq 0$.
We prove that, when this coupling is controlled by the Lasry-Lions monotonicity
of the game — a condition we call the \emph{GAN-tractable regime} — the
fixed-point iteration is a contraction in the Wasserstein-2 metric,
with an explicit geometric convergence rate.
This yields the first rigorous characterization of when GAN-based methods
provably outperform physics-informed approaches, exposing a sharp
\emph{phase transition} between the two regimes.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Weakly non-separable Hamiltonians.}
    We introduce a formal interpolation between separable and non-separable
    MFGs via a coupling parameter $\eps$, and identify the GAN-tractable
    regime $\eps L_R < \lambda$ as the first rigorous criterion separating
    the two computational regimes.

  \item \textbf{Extended-MFGAN algorithm.}
    We propose a fixed-point GAN iteration that decomposes the non-separable
    MFG into a sequence of separable subproblems, each solved by an existing
    GAN solver (e.g., APAC-Net). The algorithm reduces exactly to APAC-Net
    when $\eps = 0$.

  \item \textbf{Wasserstein contraction theorem.}
    We prove via a Lasry-Lions duality argument that the outer fixed-point
    iteration is a $\kappa$-contraction in $\mathcal{W}_2$ with explicit
    rate $\kappa = \eps L_R/\lambda < 1$, yielding the first Wasserstein
    convergence guarantee with an explicit rate for any deep learning MFG
    solver in the non-separable setting.

  \item \textbf{Sharpness of the GAN-tractable condition.}
    We prove that the rate $\kappa = \eps L_R/\lambda$ is tight in the
    bilinear case, and that the condition $\eps L_R < \lambda$ is necessary:
    we construct an explicit ergodic MFG for which the outer iteration
    diverges from every starting point as soon as $\eps L_R \geq \lambda$.

  \item \textbf{Numerical validation.}
    Across three experiments (separable limit, bilinear MFG-LWR, density-only
    sweep), the empirical contraction rate matches the theoretical rate
    $\kappa = \eps L_R/\lambda$ to four digits, including a non-trivial
    two-sided phase transition predicted for the bilinear MFG-LWR.
\end{enumerate}
