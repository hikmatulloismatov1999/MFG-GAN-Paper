\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced weakly non-separable Hamiltonians and the Extended-MFGAN
algorithm, which bridges the gap between GAN-based and physics-informed
solvers for Mean Field Games. The central contribution is a Wasserstein-2
contraction theorem that provides the first explicit convergence rate for
MFGs with non-separable Hamiltonians, and identifies a sharp criterion
separating the regime where GAN methods are provably superior from the
regime where physics-informed methods should be preferred.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The GAN-tractable condition $\eps L_R < \lambda$ is sufficient for
contraction but may not be necessary; the exact boundary of the GAN-tractable
regime remains an open question. The analysis also assumes exact inner GAN
solves; the bias introduced by finite inner iterations is bounded in
\Cref{prop:inexact}, but a tighter treatment would be of interest.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Several extensions are natural.
When $\eps L_R \geq \lambda$, one can replace the inner GAN solver with
MFDGM to form a hybrid algorithm that handles the fully non-separable regime
at the cost of weaker convergence guarantees.
Extension to mean field control problems \citep{carmona2018probabilistic},
where the social planner optimizes over the whole population, is also
of interest.
Finally, applying Extended-MFGAN to large-scale multi-agent reinforcement
learning and to engineering systems modeled by congestion-type MFGs
are promising practical directions.
