\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Mean Field Games}

Let $\Omega \subset \R^d$ be a bounded domain and $T > 0$ a finite horizon.
A Mean Field Game is described by the coupled PDE system
\eqref{eq:HJB}--\eqref{eq:FP} with initial condition
$\rho(0,\cdot) = \rho_0$ and terminal condition
$\varphi(T,\cdot) = g(\cdot, \rho(T,\cdot))$.
The solution $(\varphi, \rho)$ represents a Nash equilibrium: no agent
can improve their cost by unilaterally changing their control.

\subsection{Separable Hamiltonians and GAN-Based Methods}

When the Hamiltonian has \emph{separable} structure
\[
  H(x,\rho,p) = H_0(x,p) - f_0(x,\rho),
\]
the MFG system admits a primal-dual variational formulation via the
Hopf formula in density space \citep{benamou2000computational}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:saddle}
  \inf_{\rho}\, \sup_{\varphi}\;
  \Bigl\{
    \E_{z\sim P(z),\, t\sim\mathrm{Unif}[0,T]}
    \bigl[\partial_t\varphi + \nu\Delta\varphi - H_0(\cdot,\nabla\varphi)\bigr]
    + \E_z[\varphi(0,\cdot)] - \E_{x\sim\rho_T}[\varphi(T,x)]
    + \E[f_0(\cdot,\rho)]
  \Bigr\}.
\end{equation}
The min-max structure of \eqref{eq:saddle} matches the
Kantorovich-Rubinstein dual formulation of Wasserstein GANs
\citep{villani2009optimal}, enabling the GAN-based algorithms:

\paragraph{APAC-Net \citep{lin2021apac}.}
Uses a generator neural network to parameterize $\rho$ (sampling-based)
and a critic network to parameterize $\varphi$, trained via adversarial
updates to solve \eqref{eq:saddle}. Achieves Wasserstein-1 convergence.

\paragraph{MFGAN \citep{ruthotto2020machine}.}
Similar adversarial framework, leverages the connection to
Wasserstein-2 optimal transport.

\paragraph{Limitation.}
Both methods require separability of $H$. For non-separable $H$,
the Hopf formula does not apply and \eqref{eq:saddle} cannot be derived.

\subsection{Non-Separable Hamiltonians: MFDGM}

\citet{assouline2023deep} propose the MFDGM method, which uses two
neural networks $N_\omega$ and $N_\theta$ to directly approximate
$(\varphi, \rho)$ by minimizing the PDE residuals:
\begin{align*}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{HJB}} &= \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B
    \bigl|\partial_t\varphi_\omega + \nu\Delta\varphi_\omega
    - H(x_b,\rho_\theta,\nabla\varphi_\omega)\bigr|^2
    + \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^S
    \bigl|\varphi_\omega(T,x_s) - g(x_s,\rho_\theta(T,x_s))\bigr|^2,\\
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FP}} &= \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B
    \bigl|\partial_t\rho_\theta - \nu\Delta\rho_\theta
    - \div(\rho_\theta\,\nabla_p H)\bigr|^2
    + \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^S
    \bigl|\rho_\theta(0,x_s) - \rho_0(x_s)\bigr|^2.
\end{align*}
MFDGM works for any $H$ (separable or not) but
(i) approximates $\rho$ \emph{pointwise} rather than distributionally, and
(ii) achieves only $L^p$ ($p<2$) convergence (Theorem~3.2 of \citep{assouline2023deep}),
weaker than the Wasserstein-1 guarantee of APAC-Net.

\subsection{Comparison: Where GANs Are Better}

We summarize the formal gaps between the two families:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Method & Non-sep.\ $H$ & Stochastic & $\rho$ representation & Convergence metric \\
\midrule
APAC-Net / MFGAN & No  & Yes & Distributional (generator) & Wasserstein-1 \\
MFDGM            & Yes & Yes & Pointwise NN               & $L^p$, $p<2$  \\
\textbf{Ours}    & Weak & Yes & Distributional (generator) & Wasserstein-2 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Comparison of MFG deep learning methods.}
\label{tab:comparison}
\end{table}

\noindent The key observation is that GANs achieve \emph{distributional} learning
of $\rho$ (the generator samples from it) and \emph{Wasserstein} convergence,
both strictly stronger than MFDGM's pointwise approximation and $L^p$ convergence.
The gap is the restriction to separable $H$. Our work closes this gap
for the weakly non-separable regime.
