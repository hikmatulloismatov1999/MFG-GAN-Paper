Deep learning solvers for Mean Field Games split into two families with
a fundamental tension: GAN-based methods achieve strong Wasserstein convergence
and learn population densities distributionally, but are provably limited to
Hamiltonians with separable structure; physics-informed methods such as MFDGM
handle non-separable Hamiltonians but sacrifice both distributional learning
and Wasserstein guarantees.
We resolve this tension by introducing the class of \emph{weakly non-separable
Hamiltonians} — those where the non-separable coupling is controlled by a
small parameter — and proposing \textbf{Extended-MFGAN}, a fixed-point GAN
iteration that handles this class while retaining the full power of GAN-based
solvers.
Our main theoretical contribution is a \emph{Wasserstein-2 contraction theorem}:
we prove that Extended-MFGAN converges geometrically to the Nash equilibrium
with an explicit rate governed by the ratio of coupling strength to
Lasry-Lions monotonicity.
This yields the first formal characterization of the regime in which GAN methods
provably outperform physics-informed approaches, identifying a sharp phase
transition between the two regimes.
We validate the method on the MFG-LWR traffic flow benchmark — the canonical
non-separable problem — in both deterministic and stochastic settings,
and recover existing GAN solvers as an exact special case.

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Mean Field Games, Generative Adversarial Networks,
Non-Separable Hamiltonians, Wasserstein Convergence, Lasry-Lions Theory,
Traffic Flow.
